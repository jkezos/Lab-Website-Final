About the Lab

Our research group uses Drosophila melanogaster (fruit flies) as a model system to investigate key questions in evolutionary physiology, cardiac function, stress resistance, diet, and longevity.

Led by Dr. James Kezos, the lab integrates experimental evolution and comparative physiology with the extensive genetic, molecular, and high-throughput screening tools available in Drosophila.

Research Focus Areas

  • Exploring the relationship between heart health in Drosophila and multi-omic patterns and variations.
  • Investigating how acute and chronic stress exposure influences physiology and life-history traits.
  • Utilizing Drosophila as a model to study atrial fibrillation and other cardiac rhythm disorders.

Current Projects

Improving Heart Function and Lifespan in Obese Fruit Fly Populations via Exercise

  • Obesity and cardiovascular disease represent compounding global epidemics, with high-fat diets and excessive lipid accumulation serving as primary drivers of metabolic dysfunction and heart-related mortality.
  • Fruit flies serves as a highly efficient, genetically tractable model for evolutionary physiology studies .
  • Utilizing starvation resistance as a selective evolutionary pressure, a ten-fold replicated set of obese Drosophila populations was established in 2015 (significant increase in lipid content, cardiac arrest rate and mortality rate).
  • We focus on (1) evaluating whether physical exercise can rescue obesity-related dysfunction, and (2) identifying multi-omic signatures associated with these phenotypes across ages.
  • Completing age-specific multi-omic profiles of our obese populations could help with identify novel targets or mechanisms for human therapeutic and diagnostic measures.

Click to read more

Cardiovascular disease and heart-related defects are the leading causes of death among Western patients. Secondarily obesity has grown to epidemic proportions in the United States, with excessive lipid accumulation being a risk factor for metabolic disorders and heart disease. Unsurprisingly, the spread of high fat diets among populations around the world has been associated with the increasing rate of obesity. Drosophila (fruit flies) is an efficient model system to study heart function, heart disease, and obesity-related disorders. Drosophila are the simplest model organism that can be used for heart function studies. Similar to humans, fruit flies experience a decline in heart function and robustness with age or high fat and sugar diets. Many genes involved in cardiac development, function and disease are conserved between flies and humans. This is just a handful of many parallels between the fruit fly heart and the human heart. Additionally, the short generation-time and ease of maintenance of large fly populations, their available genomic data, as well as prior physiological research makes the fruit fly a model system especially suited to studies of physiology and disease. Evolutionary physiologists can take advantage of stress, using it as a tool either to measure physical robustness, or to create differentiated populations to study adaptation and physiological disease. Through selection for increased starvation resistance, we established a ten-fold replicated set of obese fruit fly populations. Young, obese flies exhibit increased metabolite contents, cardiac dysfunction and mortality rates compared to control populations. Genomic sequencing revealed hundreds of genes associated with these obesity-associated traits, however it is challenging to assign causality. The main foci of this research include 1) whether or not we can improve heart function and lifespan of these obese flies via exercise, and 2) what underlying multi-omic signatures are associated with obesity-related phenotypes, and do these change as a response to exercise and/or age. Identifying multi-omic signatures associated with our obese populations, and the potential to rescue the physiological dysfunction, will isolate key biological mechanisms underlying obesity and heart disease, potentially improving our own human health.

Collaborator(s) Dr. Michael Rose Dr. Mark Phillips

Evolutionary and Physiological Responses to High-Sugar Diets in Drosophila melanogaster

  • Beyond genetics and healthcare access, a major driver of obesity and diabetes is the "western diet," which is heavily defined by high sugar and high fat content.
  • Both human and animal studies confirm that high-fat and high-sugar diets lead to cardiac dysfunction, decreased physical ability, and shortened lifespans.
  • Some research indicates that long-term adaptation to a high-sugar diet can actually mitigate these negative health and fitness consequences.
  • This multi-generational study uses fruit flies to track evolutionary changes, with Dr. Phillips' lab (in Oregon) analyzing high-sugar tolerance selection alongside genomic, transcriptomic, and metabolomic shifts.
  • My team's specific focus uses high-speed video microscopy to measure cardiac structure and function, alongside stress and metabolite assays

Diabetes and Obesity are two of the top 10 causes of death, and the occurrence rate of these two conditions is growing in epidemic proportions. In addition to potential genetic predispositions and access to healthcare, a major factor to the onset of obesity and diabetes is the “western” diet. A diet that is high in both sugar and fat content. It has been shown in both humans and animal research models, these high-fat and high-sugar diets lead to cardiac dysfunction, reduced physical ability, and shortened lifespans. However, some studies have shown that adaptation to high-sugar diets can mitigate these negative fitness consequences. This collaborative, multi-lab experiment is examining how subjecting large, replicate populations of fruit flies to high sugar diets for many generations will affect physiological and molecular function. In Oregon, Dr. Mark Phillips and his lab are employing high-sugar tolerance selection techniques and examining the acute and evolutionary effects on life-history traits, the genome, the transcriptome, and the metabolome. Our lab is focusig on using high-speed video microscopy on dissected fruit flies to measure potential differences in cardiac function and structure between the ancestral and high-sugar adapted populations. Our team is also performing physiological assays (stress resistance assays and metabolite measurements) on these two sets of populations when fed either a normal, standard diet or a diet with 7X the amount of sugar. We are interested in asking 1) what are the physiological and molecular evolutionary responses to high-sugar diets, and 2) do high-sugar adapted flies survive and perform better when fed high-sugar diets? Finding these connections will help us explore and understand the molecular reasons not only behind high-sugar tolerance, but also obesity and diabetes related phenotypes.

A Novel Age-Related Role for the Developmental Hedgehog Signaling Pathway in Drosophila Hearts

  • Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most prevalent cardiac rhythm disorder in populations over 65, however the mechanisms initiating and maintaining AF remain unresolved.
  • Utilizing a collaborative translational screen of 52 human AF patient-derived candidate genes, we observed potential cardiac-relevant effects in human atrial-like cardiomyocytes and Drosophila.
  • Analysis of corroborated hits highlighted the evolutionary conserved Hedgehog (Hh) signaling pathway as a potential key mediator of age-dependent cardiac maintenance.
  • Our current investigation evaluates cardiac-specific RNAi knockdown of distinct Hh pathway components in our fly model.
  • Initial results demonstrate component-specific variations that alter adult heart function and rhythm, highlighting a complex regulatory architecture.
  • Future studies aim to expand on age-specific and sex-specific candidate profiling via stage-specific developmental knockdowns (i.e. larval phase vs adult phase).

Click to read more

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common cardiac rhythm disorder nearly affecting 10% of individuals over the age of 65 years. AF is considered a significant public health problem as it is the leading cause of heart failure and stroke. AF risk factors include age, high blood pressure, genetics and environmental factors in both human and model systems. However, it is not well understood how the risk factors synergize to promote arrhythmia, nor which malfunctioning genetic and molecular mechanisms are responsible for initiating and maintaining AF. The fly has been proven to be a useful model in elucidating conserved genetic networks of cardiac development, ageing and adult heart function. Through a collaborative effort at my PI’s former lab, they tested 52 candidate genes derived from human AF patients (Lyon Hospices De Civil) by knocking down their expression levels in the fluorescently labeled Drosophila cardiac-ageing model and the human atrial like cardiomyocyte model. Analyzing and networking the Drosophila and human cell results identified the Hedgehog signaling pathway as being potentially involved in age-related cardiac function and maintenance. My team and I are investigating the cardiac-specific knockdown of different components of the Hedgehog signaling pathway in 2 week old and 4 week old male and female flies. There appears to be age-specific and sex-specific effects for different components of this pathway, implying a novel but complicated role(s) in adult heart function. We are conducting additional experiments where we will test other components of this pathway, we will also knock down these pathway components during specific development stages, as well as verify the knockdown efficiency of these components.

Collaborator(s) Dr. Karen Ocorr Dr. Rolf Bodmer Dr. Alexandre Colas Dr. Philippe Chevalier

Identifying neuronal clusters that influence the Drosophila pacemaker cells

  • While the myogenic human heart beats on its own, it does not act in isolation and is heavily influenced by external neural inputs.
  • Mapping these neurocardiac pathways in humans is incredibly difficult due to the brain's 86 billion neurons; Drosophila melanogaster offers a vastly simpler model with fewer than 200,000 neurons.
  • Unlike the human heart, the fruit fly's rhythm is controlled by two distinct pacemaker regions located along its cardiac tube.
  • Although both human and fruit fly hearts are known to be innervated by neurons, the specific types of neurons involved and their exact molecular effects remain poorly understood.
  • This study uses the GAL4/UAS system alongside blue-light-activated channelrhodopsin to stimulate specific neuronal types and observe how they affect heart function and rhythm.
  • The project aims to identify how specific neuron clusters alter heart rate and heartbeat directionality.

Click to read more

Despite the heart’s central role in sustaining life, it is imperative to understand that the myogenic human heart does not beat in isolation - but receives influence from neural input. Isolating and understanding human neurocardiac pathways is incredibly difficult as there are approximately 86 billion neurons within the human brain, and vertebrate models can be complex due to the duplicitous roles of many genes and proteins. However, Drosophila melanogaster has under 200,000 neurons, a drastically reduced number allowing for effective and efficient experimentation and data collection. The human heartbeat relies on a pacemaker region within the sinoatrial node, while Drosophila’s rhythmicity relies on two pacemaker regions along the cardiac tube. Both the human and Drosophila heart have been observed to be innervated by neurons; however, the type of neurons and specific effects remain poorly understood due to the limited molecular approaches. This research aims to address which neuronal clusters influence (directly or indirectly) the pacemaker region(s) in Drosophila, therefore affecting heart rhythm. The GAL4/UAS system coupled with blue-light activated channel rhodopsin will be used to identify which neuronal type(s) are associated within the neurocardiac pathway. The fluorescently labeled cardiac tube of the fly will be recorded before, during and after blue-light activation of the corresponding neurons to document potential changes in cardiac rhythm and function parameters. The overall objective is to determine the relationship between specific neuronal types and Drosophila cardiac pacemaker cells via changes in Drosophila heart rate and heartbeat directionality.

Microbiome Composition Analyses of Obese Drosophila Populations Experiencing Heightened Early-Age Mortality

  • Obesity is marked by increased adipose (fat) tissue, which drives critical health risks including Type II Diabetes, cardiovascular disorders, and chronic inflammation.
  • Inflammatory responses disrupt the gut microbiome and immune function; this dysregulation then feeds back to promote further insulin resistance and inflammation, reinforcing cardiovascular risks.
  • The gut microbiome can act as a critical link between metabolic and cardiovascular health.
  • To study these complex interrelationships, we are using our lab's 10 outbred obese fruit fly populations which exhibit severe fitness costs compared to controls.
  • Our students are collecting Drosophila guts and malpighian tubules from obese and control populations in order to extract and analyze microbial composition (collaboration with Dr. Elinne Becket)

Click to read more

Obesity is a global issue characterized by an increased level of adipose tissue in the body, which contributes to health risks such as Type II Diabetes, cardiovascular disorders and chronic inflammation. These inflammatory responses have been indicated in disrupted gut microbiome and immune function. This dysregulation of the gut microbiome can continue to promote inflammation and insulin resistance, which reinforces the cardiovascular risks and obesity. Because of the interconnected processes, the gut microbiome is recognized in literature as a factor that links cardiovascular disorders, Type II Diabetes, and overall health. Drosophila melanogaster, the fruit fly model system, is a novel approach to studying these interconnected relationships.

Our lab has a set 10 outbred obese populations that display higher early age mortality, three times higher lipid content, and elevated cardiac arrest rates after electrical pacing compared to ancestral populations. With our collaborators, we are now characterizing the multi-omic differences (i.e. genomic, transcriptomic, proteomic, metabolomic, and metagenomic) of these 10 obese populations and the respective control populations. We are in process of collecting the intestines and Malpighian tubules (MTs) of male and female flies from the 10 obese populations and 10 control/ancestral populations at different ages. We will then be extracting, sequencing and analyzing microbial DNA (16s rRNA sequencing and whole-genome sequencing approaches). For this project, we are interested in asking the following two questions: 1) does the microbial diversity and abundance differ between obese and control populations and 2) does microbial diversity and abundance change between age and sex?

Collaborator(s) Dr. Elinne Becket

Investigating the Effects of Aspartame on Drosophila Physiology and Lifespan

  • The use of artificial sweeteners, such as Aspartame, has significantly increased in the past decade to combat obesity and obesity-associated diseases.
  • Aspartame is a non-nutritive sweetener approximately 180-200 times sweeter than normal sugar and is widely used in, but not limited to, diet products.
  • While considered generally safe by regulatory agencies like the Food and Drug Administration, emerging research suggests that Aspartame may have a potential link to neurological, metabolic, and cardiovascular health risks.
  • Flies fed control diet had increased early-age mortality rates and an overall decrease in lifespan compared to flies fed the Aspartame diets (lower dosages).
  • Aspartame diets had minimal effects on cardiac function or motor function with a slight trend towards reduced cardiac arrhythmicity.
  • Unlike humans, Aspartame triggers Drosophila bitter taste receptors, so possibly witnessing the effects of caloric restriction.

Dr. James Kezos (Ph.D. Biological Sciences, University of California Irvine)

Dr. Kezos joined the Biology faculty at CSUSM in Fall 2024 after conducting his postdoctoral research at Sanford Burnham Prebys Medical Discovery Institute. During his doctoral studies at UC Irvine (2011-2017), Dr. Kezos employed techniques and tools associated with experimental evolution to study cardiac physiology, stress resistance, and life-history of different experimentally-evolved Drosophila populations (Mentors: Dr. Michael Rose and Dr. Laurence Mueller). His current lab is incorporating a blend of the different experimental approaches and techniques from his previous trainings in efforts to better improve our understanding of how cardiac function and disorder is closely intertwined with age, stress, and metabolism.

Super STEM Saturday

Super STEM Saturday is a one-day festival held in March that engages kids of all ages and their families to the diverse and exciting fields of science, technology, engineering and mathematics, known as the STEM disciplines. Held in partnership with the College of Science, Technology, Engineering & Mathematics at CSUSM and The Classical Academies, Super STEM Saturday has brought science to life for more than 50,000 people in North San Diego County.

Our lab has been participating in this event since March 2026. We run a booth called "The Fly Factory" where children and adults will learn about how scientists use physical markers (i.e. curly wings or short bristles) to keep track of genetic mutations. We also have microscopes where people can look at different fruit flies. Children can also make their own fruit fly by choosing body color, wing color and type, eye colors and other fun decorations.

Military Children Outreach Events

San Diego is home to a large military and military-family community. Many organizations around the county host events to support these families, especially their children. Our lab and students have also volunteered at multiple events hosted at The Steven A. Cohen Military Family Clinic, running different science-themed interactive booths. One of the groups we have previously volunteered with is Blue Star Families (July 2025). Their events are specifically tailored to support military-connected children and families, focusing on building community, relieving deployment stress, and offering free recreational experiences.

K-12 Lab Tours and Activities

Our laboratory provides educational tours and interactive demonstrations designed to engage elementary, middle school, and high school students in biological and fruit-fly related concepts. Led by our undergraduate researchers, visiting students observe diverse Drosophila melanogaster (fruit fly) populations, utilize laboratory microscopes to view physical differences between fly populations, and observe live filming of the fruit fly heart. Depending on time and age, visiting students can help separate flies under the microscope or help prepare flies on a microscope slide to film their hearts. Additionally, our undergraduate students periodically present their ongoing research posters to these visiting classes. Lastly, interested students can design their own fruit fly magnet by choosing body color, wing color, wing shape, eye color, and other decorations as a artsy lesson about fly physical markers (balancers). Through peer mentorship and direct exposure to a university laboratory setting, this outreach aims to foster early interest in higher education and demonstrate the accessibility of undergraduate scientific research.

If you and your class or organization are intersted in a lab tour, or have an outreach event looking for science-related booths and activities, please feel free to contact James Kezos (contact information below).
